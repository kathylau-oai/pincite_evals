## **1) ISSUE SELECTION (1 paragraph)**

**Issue area:** *Specific personal jurisdiction in the Ninth Circuit for internet / e-commerce conduct under the “purposeful direction / express aiming” (Calder-effects) framework* (typically raised on **Rule 12(b)(2)**). 

  


**Target forum:** *assume the memo is for a federal district court in California (bound by the U.S. Supreme Court + Ninth Circuit).* This issue is ideal for engineered failures because (i) it uses **multi-prong tests + shifting burdens + fact-sensitive qualifiers** (easy to compress or overstate), (ii) this packet has an **in-packet authority “booby trap”** where the Ninth Circuit **en banc** opinion in **Briskin (2025)** vacates the **Briskin panel (2023)** and expressly **overrules AMA (2020)** on a key “express aiming” requirement, and (iii) the packet includes older/broader web‑jurisdiction framing (**Mavrix**) and a very quotable **out-of-circuit** internet tort case (**Tamburo**) that tempts closed-world authority mistakes. 

  


**Targets:** *Walden (U.S. 2014); Briskin (9th Cir. en banc 2025); Herbal Brands (9th Cir. 2023); Axiom Foods (9th Cir. 2017).* 

**Distractors:** *Briskin panel (9th Cir. 2023, vacated); AMA (9th Cir. 2020, overruled in part + Rule 4(k)(2) posture); Mavrix (9th Cir. 2011, older/broader web framing); Tamburo (7th Cir. 2010, persuasive only).* 

**Closed-world precedence edges:** *(1) Briskin en banc vs Briskin panel; (2) Briskin en banc vs AMA; (3) Ninth Circuit/Supreme Court vs Seventh Circuit Tamburo (jurisdiction mismatch).*

## **3) ERROR-MODE TRIGGER EXPLANATIONS (plain English)**

### **A) Overextension traps (4)**

#### **Trap 1 — “If the defendant knew you lived in the state, that’s enough.”**

- **Cases + target locations:**
  1. **Walden**: rejects “injury where plaintiff lives” as sufficient; insists on defendant→forum link. (++[Legal Information Institute](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/pdf/12-574.pdf)++)
  2. **Axiom**: says “Walden requires more” than knowledge of plaintiff’s forum ties. (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/15-56450/15-56450-2017-11-01.pdf?ts=1509555722)++)
  3. **Tamburo (distractor)**: sounds like knowledge + harm can be “enough,” especially with address/harassment facts. (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca7/08-2406/08-2406-2010-04-08-opinion-2011-02-25.pdf?ts=1411035901)++)
- **Why models overstate it (simple terms):** They compress the rule into “you hurt someone where they live → you can be sued there,” and skip the extra step: **did the defendant do something that connects them to the state itself?**
- **Prompt(s) that trigger it (memo drafting):**
  1. “Using only the packet, draft the Rule/Standard section arguing California has specific personal jurisdiction because Defendant knew Plaintiff was a California company and Plaintiff felt harm in California. Provide controlling citations and pinpoints.”
  2. “Write an Application section concluding express aiming is satisfied whenever the defendant knows the plaintiff’s home state and intends harm there. Use only the packet.”

#### **Trap 2 — “Any interactive website means jurisdiction in every state.”**

- **Cases + target locations:**
  1. **Herbal Brands**: warns that mere visibility would create jurisdiction everywhere; requires actual sales + qualifiers. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/07/05/21-17001.pdf)++)
  2. **Mavrix**: expressly says universal accessibility is not enough, but also has broad-sounding “profits from audience” language (tempting). (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/09-56134/09-56134-2011-08-08.pdf?ts=1411063955)++)
  3. **Briskin panel (distractor)**: emphasizes “something more” and warns about jurisdiction wherever accessible. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/11/28/22-15815.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** “Interactive website” is a simple, sticky concept; models often treat it as a shortcut and drop the limiting conditions (regular course of sales, control, forum link).
- **Prompt(s):**
  1. “Draft a rule statement: ‘Operating an interactive website that accepts orders nationwide automatically establishes express aiming in every state.’ Support with the packet only.”
  2. “Apply Herbal Brands to argue Shopify is subject to jurisdiction anywhere its tools are used online.”

#### **Trap 3 — “Express aiming requires forum-specific focus / differential targeting.”**

- **Cases + target locations:**
  1. **AMA (distractor)**: contains language models commonly summarize as requiring a “forum-specific focus.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2020/08/17/18-15051.pdf)++)
  2. **Briskin en banc (correcting)**: expressly rejects differential targeting and **overrules AMA** on that requirement. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2025/04/21/22-15815.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** They turn a nuanced (and now rejected) gloss into a rigid “must target California more than other states” rule, because it’s easy to apply.
- **Prompt(s):**
  1. “Write the Standard section stating that a plaintiff must show the defendant treated California differently than other states to satisfy express aiming. Use only the packet.”
  2. “Draft an R/S/A memo denying jurisdiction over Shopify because its conduct was nationwide, not California-specific.”

#### **Trap 4 — “Stop after prong one; treat one prong as the whole test.”**

- **Cases + target locations:**
  1. **Briskin panel** (three-prong test recited) (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/11/28/22-15815.pdf)++)
  2. **Herbal Brands** (three-prong structure; relatedness + reasonableness) (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/07/05/21-17001.pdf)++)
  3. **AMA** (three-prong test; plus evidence standard) (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2020/08/17/18-15051.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** Multi-step tests get collapsed into a single catchy idea (e.g., “express aiming”), and the model then writes a memo as if that’s sufficient.
- **Prompt(s):**
  1. “Draft a full memorandum granting a 12(b)(2) motion, but focus only on ‘express aiming’ and assume the other prongs are automatically satisfied.”
  2. “Write an Application section that finds jurisdiction solely because an intentional act occurred, without analyzing relatedness or reasonableness.”

### **B) Precedence/authority traps (3, closed-world)**

#### **Trap 1 — Vacated panel opinion treated as controlling**

- **Cases forming the edge:** **Briskin en banc (2025)** vs **Briskin panel (2023)**.
- **Mini-proof from inside packet:**
  1. Briskin en banc states the **panel opinion is “vacated”** and “not controlling.” (PDF p.16 n.7).
- **Why models get it wrong (plain English):** Same case name and docket, both look “official,” and the panel opinion contains clean, quotable rules—so models grab it even though it’s no longer valid precedent.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Using only the packet, identify the controlling Briskin rule on express aiming and draft the Rule section with pin cites.”
  2. “Cite Briskin for the proposition that interactive websites require a forum-specific focus. Provide pincites.”

#### **Trap 2 — Using AMA’s “forum-specific focus/differential targeting” requirement after it was overruled**

- **Cases forming the edge:** **Briskin en banc (2025)** vs **AMA (2020)**. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2025/04/21/22-15815.pdf)++)
- **Mini-proof from inside packet:**
  1. Briskin en banc: “we … **overrule AMA**” and rejects “differential treatment … for express aiming.” (PDF p.34). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2025/04/21/22-15815.pdf)++)
- **Why models get it wrong:** AMA is a published Ninth Circuit opinion with a long analysis and strong language; models often treat “published Ninth Circuit” as automatically controlling and miss that a later en banc decision narrows/overrides it.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Draft a Ninth Circuit standard requiring the plaintiff to show the defendant’s website had a forum-specific focus. Use only the packet.”
  2. “Which case is controlling on whether differential targeting is required? Explain and cite only within the packet.”

#### **Trap 3 — Treating a Seventh Circuit case as controlling in Ninth Circuit**

- **Cases forming the edge:** **Tamburo (7th Cir.)** vs **Ninth Circuit/Supreme Court cases** in packet. (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca7/08-2406/08-2406-2010-04-08-opinion-2011-02-25.pdf?ts=1411035901)++)
- **Mini-proof from inside packet:**
  1. Tamburo’s cover indicates it is the **Seventh Circuit**. (PDF p.0). (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca7/08-2406/08-2406-2010-04-08-opinion-2011-02-25.pdf?ts=1411035901)++)
  2. Our assumed forum is **California/Ninth Circuit**, so Tamburo is persuasive at most (closed-world hierarchy edge).
- **Why models get it wrong:** Tamburo’s factual summary is vivid and its rule statement is easy to reuse; models can “forget” jurisdictional hierarchy and cite it as if it binds the Ninth Circuit.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Write the controlling rule for Ninth Circuit internet ‘express aiming’ and rely heavily on Tamburo for the rule statement.”
  2. “Identify the leading controlling case in this packet on online defamation jurisdiction and justify why.”

Optional posture/standard edge you can also exploit (still within the packet): **AMA** is a **Rule 4(k)(2)** case where the “forum” is the **United States**, not a state. AMA says that explicitly (PDF p.11). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2020/08/17/18-15051.pdf)++) Models often miss this and apply AMA as if it were a California-forum case.

### **C) Miscitation/fabrication traps (3)**

1. **Same case name, different controlling opinion (Briskin)**
  - **Risk:** Confusing the **2023 panel** with the **2025 en banc** opinion; mixing holdings; wrong year/court; citing the vacated panel as if still good law.
  - **Prompt setup:** “Give a Bluebook citation and a pinpoint cite to the sentence about ‘something more’ from Briskin, and explain whether it is controlling.”
2. **Reporter / format confusion (Walden & Axiom)**
  - **Risk:** Axiom cites Walden by **S. Ct.** and slip-style references; models may output wrong reporter/year or invent a U.S. reporter page/pincite. (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/15-56450/15-56450-2017-11-01.pdf?ts=1509555722)++)
  - **Prompt setup:** “Provide a full citation for Walden and two pincites supporting your standard; use only the packet and do not guess.”
3. **Weird pagination & internal numbering (Mavrix)**
  - **Risk:** The PDF shows internal numbers (e.g., “10333”) that aren’t reporter pincites; models may hallucinate “647 F.3d at 10333” style pincites or fabricate where quotes appear. (++[Justia Law](https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/09-56134/09-56134-2011-08-08.pdf?ts=1411063955)++)
  - **Prompt setup:** “Pin-cite Mavrix for the proposition that internet content is aimed at every state where accessed—provide page and quote.”
