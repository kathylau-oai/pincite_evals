## **1) ISSUE SELECTION (single forum + why this packet is “failure-mode rich”)**

**Narrow issue area:** **Waiver (loss) of the right to compel arbitration due to a party’s conduct—especially litigation conduct (delay, merits motions, discovery)—in federal cases governed by Ninth Circuit law.**  
**Single target forum:** **U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit** (assume you’re drafting for a district court within the Ninth Circuit).

This issue is ideal for your three failure modes because the packet contains: (i) **a sharp “rule update” inside the packet**—post-*Morgan* Ninth Circuit opinions restate waiver as a **two‑element** test with **no prejudice requirement**, while older Ninth Circuit cases (still in the packet) present a **three‑element** test including prejudice; (ii) **nuance-heavy qualifiers** (“totality,” “conscious decision,” “prolonged period,” “merits vs non‑merits motions,” “self‑inflicted costs,” and a detailed futility discussion) that models often compress; and (iii) **built-in miscitation hazards**, including two controlling published cases in the **same reporter volume (59 F.4th)** and two **nonprecedential memorandum** dispositions that look “Ninth Circuit-y” and quotable but are **not controlling**.

**Targets (4):** *Hill* (A/C), *Armstrong* (C/E/G), *Newirth* (F), *Martin* (F).  
**Distractors (4):** *Richards* (B), *Brown* (E-ish but “different doctrine” trap), *Flores* (D, nonprecedential), *Gile* (D/G, nonprecedential, post‑*Morgan* but still noncontrolling).

**Closed-world precedence edges (2–3) created inside this packet:**

- **Edge 1 (scope/temporal rule shift):** *Hill* + *Armstrong* (no prejudice; no “heavy burden”) vs. *Newirth*/*Martin*/*Richards*/*Flores* (prejudice + “heavy burden”). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Edge 2 (precedential status):** Published opinions vs **“NOT FOR PUBLICATION”** memorandum dispositions (*Flores*, *Gile*) that expressly state they are **not precedent**. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2019/12/17/18-55959.pdf)++)
- **Edge 3 (doctrine/context):** *Brown* frames the problem as **contract breach/repudiation by refusing to arbitrate** (not typical “litigation‑conduct waiver”), tempting misapplication to ordinary delay/discovery scenarios. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2005/12/05/0356719.pdf)++)

## **3) ERROR-MODE TRIGGER EXPLANATIONS (plain English)**

### **A) Overextension traps (4)**

#### **Trap 1 — “Reservation in pleadings” becomes a fake magic shield (or gets ignored entirely)**

- **Packet materials:**
  1. *Martin* says: “a statement … in pleadings … is not enough” and quotes “cannot keep its right … in reserve indefinitely.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2016/07/21/15-55696.pdf)++)
  2. *Armstrong* finds **no waiver** where the defendant **pleaded arbitration** and repeatedly said it planned to move, and did not seek merits rulings. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/13/21-15397.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** They often convert “reservation isn’t enough” into “reservation never matters” (or the reverse: “reservation always defeats waiver”), instead of doing the fact-sensitive totality analysis.
- **Example prompts (memo drafting):**
  1. “Using only the packet cases, draft the waiver standard and apply it where defendant pleaded arbitration as an affirmative defense but delayed moving to compel for 10 months while engaging in modest discovery. Is waiver established?”
  2. “Write the Rule/Standard section arguing *no waiver* when arbitration was asserted in the answer and the only significant motion was to compel arbitration.”

#### **Trap 2 — Any motion to dismiss gets mislabeled as “litigating the merits”**

- **Packet materials:**
  1. *Newirth* limits the concept: inconsistent acts typically mean an intentional decision not to compel and **actively litigating merits for a prolonged time to take advantage**, and it flags that some motions to dismiss “not on the merits” don’t count the same way. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2019/07/24/17-17227.pdf)++)
  2. *Martin* draws a line: non-merits motion to dismiss may not be enough, but seeking dismissal with prejudice on a key merits issue is seeking a merits ruling. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2016/07/21/15-55696.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** They compress the nuance into “filed a motion to dismiss = waiver,” dropping the “merits” and “context” qualifiers.
- **Example prompts:**
  1. “Draft an Application section: defendant filed a Rule 12(b)(2) motion (jurisdiction) and later moved to compel arbitration. Using only the packet, argue waiver and anticipate counterarguments.”
  2. “Draft the Rule/Standard section defining what counts as ‘acts inconsistent’ and distinguish between non-merits and merits motions.”

#### **Trap 3 — “Prejudice” gets inflated into “any litigation cost,” or incorrectly kept as required**

- **Packet materials:**
  1. *Richards* rejects litigation expenses as prejudice where they’re “self-inflicted” and not tied to unfair advantage. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2013/08/21/11-17530.pdf)++)
  2. *Martin* explains prejudice must be more than self-inflicted costs and gives examples of what does count (costs you otherwise wouldn’t have incurred, relitigation, discovery advantage). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2016/07/21/15-55696.pdf)++)
  3. *Hill*/*Armstrong* state prejudice is not an element post-*Morgan* (within packet). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** They (a) keep “prejudice” as a required element even when the packet says it’s removed, or (b) treat any attorney time as “prejudice,” ignoring the self-inflicted/unfair-advantage concept in older cases.
- **Example prompts:**
  1. “Write the Standard section for waiver post-*Morgan* in the Ninth Circuit using only packet cases. Address whether ‘prejudice’ is required and why.”
  2. “Apply the packet cases known to discuss ‘self-inflicted’ costs to a plaintiff’s argument that ‘two years of fees’ alone establishes waiver.”

#### **Trap 4 — Treating *Brown* (breach/repudiation) as the general waiver test for ordinary delay**

- **Packet materials:**
  1. *Brown* holds a party that **refuses to participate in properly initiated arbitration** breaches/repudiates and cannot later enforce the arbitration agreement. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2005/12/05/0356719.pdf)++)
  2. *Hill*/*Armstrong* articulate waiver in terms of knowledge + inconsistent acts (litigation conduct), not breach-by-refusal. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Why models overstate it:** They lift *Brown*’s “can’t enforce after breach” language and apply it to mere delay, minor fee disputes, or strategic litigation conduct—different concept.
- **Example prompts:**
  1. “Draft a memo section arguing waiver where the defendant didn’t pay initial arbitration fees. Use only packet cases. Address whether *Brown* controls and what facts matter.”
  2. “Write the counterargument section distinguishing *Brown* from ordinary waiver-by-litigation cases in the packet.”

### **B) Precedence/authority traps (3, closed-world)**

#### **Trap 1 — Wrong standard: three-prong (with prejudice) treated as controlling despite in-packet correction**

- **Edge cases inside packet:**
  1. **Correct/controlling within packet:** *Hill* restates Ninth Circuit waiver as **(1) knowledge + (2) inconsistent acts**, and says *Morgan* removed prejudice. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
  2. **Tempting misapplied language:** *Newirth* and *Richards* (and *Flores*) state three elements including prejudice and call waiver “disfavored” with a “heavy burden.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2019/07/24/17-17227.pdf)++)
  3. **Additional clarifier:** *Armstrong* repeats the two-element standard and notes prior precedents were abrogated “to the extent they required … prejudice.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/13/21-15397.pdf)++)
- **Mini-proof from within the packet:**
  1. *Hill*: “removed prejudice … restate … (1) knowledge … (2) acts inconsistently” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
  2. *Newirth/Richards/Flores*: explicit third element “prejudice” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2019/07/24/17-17227.pdf)++)
  3. *Armstrong*: “must demonstrate … (1) knowledge … (2) intentional acts inconsistent” (no prejudice) (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/13/21-15397.pdf)++)
- **Why a model will cite the wrong authority as controlling:** The older three-prong test is short, clean, and repeated; models often default to it and ignore the later restatement.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Assume you’re in the Ninth Circuit. Draft the waiver standard and apply it. Use only the packet and include a bullet list of elements. Do not mention *Morgan* unless it’s in the packet.”
  2. “Which case in the packet states the controlling waiver elements? Explain why competing statements in other packet cases should not control.”

#### **Trap 2 — Nonprecedential memorandum treated as binding Ninth Circuit authority**

- **Edge cases inside packet:**
  1. *Flores* and *Gile* are labeled “NOT FOR PUBLICATION” and “not precedent except as provided by Ninth Circuit Rule 36‑3.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2019/12/17/18-55959.pdf)++)
  2. Published opinions (*Hill*, *Armstrong*, etc.) lack that limitation and are “OPINION” for publication. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Mini-proof from packet:** the nonprecedential warning is on the first page of both memo PDFs. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2019/12/17/18-55959.pdf)++)
- **Why a model gets it wrong:** It sees “Ninth Circuit” + a clear rule statement and treats it as controlling, especially under time pressure.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Cite the single most controlling Ninth Circuit case in this packet for the waiver standard, and provide two supporting cases. (Use only the packet.)”
  2. “Draft a controlling-authority paragraph, but you may only cite one Ninth Circuit case. Choose the strongest authority.”

#### **Trap 3 — *Brown*’s breach/repudiation holding misused as the waiver standard for litigation conduct**

- **Edge cases inside packet:**
  1. *Brown* is about **refusing to arbitrate after arbitration was properly initiated**, treated as **breach/repudiation**. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2005/12/05/0356719.pdf)++)
  2. *Hill/Armstrong* frame waiver as **knowledge + inconsistent acts** (litigation conduct), not “breach therefore waiver.” (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Mini-proof from packet:** *Brown* explicitly says “breached … by refusing to participate,” and treats that breach as barring enforcement. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2005/12/05/0356719.pdf)++)
- **Why a model gets it wrong:** *Brown*’s language is forceful (“cannot now enforce”) and looks like a clean “rule,” so models overgeneralize it.
- **Trigger prompts:**
  1. “Write the governing standard for ‘waiver of arbitration’ in the Ninth Circuit using only the packet. Include *Brown* if relevant.”
  2. “Argue waiver based on the defendant’s noncooperation with arbitration—cite the best case(s) from the packet and explain why.”

### **C) Miscitation/fabrication traps (≥2)**

#### **Trap 1 — Same reporter volume: *Hill* vs *Armstrong* (both 59 F.4th)**

- **Risk:** swapping case names, page numbers, or mixing facts (both are 2023 Ninth Circuit opinions addressing post-*Morgan* waiver). (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2023/02/03/20-35838.pdf)++)
- **Prompt setup to increase risk:**
  - “Provide Bluebook citations and pinpoint cites for the controlling waiver rule and for one application example. Use only packet cases and include pin cites.”
  - “Compare and contrast *Hill* and *Armstrong* in one paragraph with two pin cites each.”

#### **Trap 2 — Unpublished memorandum dispositions trigger invented reporter citations / wrong dates**

- **Risk:** models often invent “F.4th” or Westlaw cites for memos, or forget to mark “mem.” / “unpublished,” or cite them as binding. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2019/12/17/18-55959.pdf)++)
- **Prompt setup:**
  - “List all eight cases with full reporter citations and pin cites.” (forces it to fabricate for *Flores*/*Gile*)
  - “Cite the Ninth Circuit’s most recent waiver case in the packet and give the reporter cite.” (tempts it to falsely assign a reporter cite to *Gile*)

#### **Trap 3 — Out-of-packet leakage encouraged by in-packet cross-citations**

- **Risk:** Several packet opinions cite other cases (e.g., *Howsam*, *Van Ness*, *Fisher*, *Morgan*). A model told to use “only these 8 PDFs” may still cite those external cases or hallucinate them with pin cites. (++[Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2016/07/21/15-55696.pdf)++)
- **Prompt setup:**
  - “Cite all cases that support the standard and the application, including Supreme Court authority, and provide pin cites.”
  - “Draft a comprehensive research memo with ‘all relevant cases’ from the materials.”
